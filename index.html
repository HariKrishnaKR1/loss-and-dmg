Tracking the Evolution of the Loss and Damage Financing Architecture

A Critical Discourse Analysis of the Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage (FRLD) under the UNFCCC

Read the Paper

Research Overview

Introduction

This research examines the institutional and textual evolution of Loss and Damage (L&D) finance. While L&D has gained recognition as the "third pillar" of climate action, it remains structurally underfunded.

This thesis investigates how changes in negotiating language—shifting from "liability and compensation" to "cooperation and facilitation"—have impacted the fiscal authority of the FRLD.

Executive Summary

The study finds that the FRLD's architecture is constrained by a "destructive ambiguity" that prioritizes consensus over capital mobilization.

Paragraph 51 of Decision 1/CP.21 explicitly excludes liability. This exclusion is not merely political but structural, directly limiting the Fund's legal authority to enforce mandatory contributions.

Key Research Findings

  • 01

    The Normative Shift

    UNFCCC texts from 1991 to 2024 show a systematic decline in liability-related terms in favor of voluntary cooperation, transitioning L&D from a legal obligation to a discretionary act of solidarity.

  • 02

    Structural Limitations

    The exclusion of liability in the Paris Agreement acts as a foundational legal limit, rendering financial contributions entirely discretionary and removing the juridical link to historical emissions.

  • 03

    The Revenue Authority Gap

    Total pledges as of 2025 account for less than 0.2% of the projected annual need. Without mandatory revenue-raising authority, the Fund remains structurally incapable of closing the gap.

  • 04

    Institutional Incompatibility

    Hosting by the World Bank subjects the Fund to fiduciary architectures favoring market-return logic, which is fundamentally at odds with addressing irreversible and non-economic losses.
