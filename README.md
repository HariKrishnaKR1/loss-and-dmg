# Tracking the Evolution of the Loss and Damage Financing Architecture under the UNFCCC

**Author:** K R Hari Krishna

## Introduction

This research examines the institutional and textual evolution of Loss and Damage (L&D) finance under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). While L&D has gained recognition as the "third pillar" of climate action alongside mitigation and adaptation, it remains structurally underfunded. This thesis employs Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) to investigate how changes in negotiating language—specifically the shift from "liability and compensation" to "cooperation and facilitation"—have impacted the fiscal authority of the Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage (FRLD).

## Executive Summary

The establishment of the Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage (FRLD) at COP28 was heralded as a historic achievement for climate justice. However, this study finds that the Fund's architecture is constrained by a "destructive ambiguity" that prioritizes consensus over capital mobilization.

### Key Findings

1. **The Normative Shift**: Analysis of UNFCCC texts from 1991 to 2024 reveals a systematic decline in liability-related terminology ("restitution," "polluter pays") in favor of voluntary cooperation frameworks. This shift has transitioned L&D finance from a legal obligation based on historical responsibility to a discretionary act of solidarity.
2. **Structural Limitations**: Paragraph 51 of Decision 1/CP.21 (the Paris Agreement) explicitly excludes liability and compensation. This study demonstrates that this exclusion is not merely political but structural, directly limiting the FRLD's legal authority to enforce mandatory capital contributions.
3. **The Revenue Gap**: As of 2025, the FRLD's total pledges account for less than 0.2% of the projected annual need of $400 billion. Without the authority to impose levies or mandatory assessments, the Fund remains reliant on unpredictable donor cycles.
4. **Institutional Constraints**: The interim hosting of the FRLD by the World Bank subjects the Fund to existing fiduciary architectures that favor market-return logic, which is fundamentally incompatible with the non-economic and irreversible nature of Loss and Damage.

### Conclusion

The funding gap in Loss and Damage is a fixed feature of the current legal structure. To move beyond discretionary aid, the international community must revisit the Fund's Governing Instrument to incorporate mandatory revenue-raising tools, such as solidarity levies on international aviation or shipping, which operate independently of the liability deadlock.
