## Abstract

Loss and damage (L&D) has emerged as the critical "third pillar" of the international climate regime, yet its financial architecture remains plagued by structural deficiencies and normative ambiguity. This thesis investigates the evolution of L&D financing under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) from 1991 to 2024. Utilizing Fairclough’s three-dimensional model of Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA), the study examines the transition from a liability-based paradigm to one centered on voluntary cooperation and facilitation. The research finds that the exclusion of "liability and compensation" in Paragraph 51 of Decision 1/CP.21 represents a foundational legal constraint that fundamentally limits the Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage (FRLD)’s potential to mobilize mandatory revenue. The study concludes that the current financing architecture creates a "destructive ambiguity" that prioritizes political consensus over substantive climate justice.

## 1. Introduction: The Loss and Damage Imperative

The intensification of anthropogenic climate change has led to irreversible losses and damages that exceed the adaptive capacities of vulnerable nations. While mitigation seeks to reduce emissions and adaptation aims to adjust to climate impacts, Loss and Damage (L&D) addresses the residual impacts that occur when both have failed or were not possible. Despite its urgency, L&D remained on the periphery of climate negotiations for decades.

The central research question of this thesis is: *How has the shift in the normative discourse of L&D financing—from liability to cooperation—impacted the structural capacity of the UNFCCC to mobilize finance?* By tracking the linguistic and institutional markers of this evolution, the study demonstrates that the current financial architecture is not merely underfunded but is structurally designed to remain discretionary.

## 2. Theoretical and Methodological Framework

### 2.1 Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA)
This research adopts Fairclough’s CDA framework, which posits that language is a form of social practice that both reflects and reproduces power relations. CDA is particularly suited for this study as it allows for the examination of how legal texts and COP decisions serve as sites of struggle between the Global North and Global South.

### 2.2 The Three-Dimensional Model
1. **Textual Analysis (Description)**: Analyzing the specific vocabulary and grammar of UNFCCC decisions (e.g., the transition from "compensation" to "funding arrangements").
2. **Discursive Practice (Interpretation)**: Investigating how these texts are produced and consumed within the context of international diplomacy.
3. **Social Practice (Explanation)**: Explaining the ideological shifts that necessitate these linguistic changes, specifically the protection of historical emitters from legal liability.

## 3. Historical Evolution: From Liability to Cooperation

### 3.1 The AOSIS Proposal (1991)
The evolution begins with the 1991 proposal by the Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS), which called for an international insurance pool based on the "polluter pays" principle. This initial paradigm was rooted in historical responsibility and legal liability, aiming to provide restitution for sea-level rise.

### 3.2 The Warsaw International Mechanism (2013)
Decision 2/CP.19 established the Warsaw International Mechanism (WIM), marking the first formal institutional home for L&D. However, the discourse began to shift. The WIM was placed under the Cancun Adaptation Framework, signaling a move away from the independent status of L&D and towards a facilitative, rather than compensatory, role.

### 3.3 Article 8 and Paragraph 51 of the Paris Agreement (2015)
The Paris Agreement represented a dual victory and defeat for the Global South. While Article 8 permanently enshrined L&D in international law, Paragraph 51 of the accompanying decision explicitly stated that Article 8 "does not involve or provide a basis for any liability or compensation." This study argues that Paragraph 51 is the "original sin" of the current L&D architecture, as it decoupled climate impacts from legal accountability.

## 4. Analyzing the Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage (FRLD)

### 4.1 The COP28 Breakthrough and its Discontents
The operationalization of the FRLD at COP28 was celebrated as a milestone. However, textual analysis of the Governing Instrument reveals a preference for "voluntary" contributions over "mandatory" assessments. This creates a chronic revenue-authority gap, where the Fund’s resources are subject to the political whims of donor nations.

### 4.2 The Role of the World Bank
The decision to host the FRLD interimly at the World Bank has profound implications. The World Bank’s fiduciary standards and market-based return logic are often at odds with the needs of L&D, which frequently involves non-economic losses (cultural heritage, loss of territory) that cannot be quantified or "returned" in a market sense.

## 5. Findings: The Normative Erosion of Climate Justice

The research identifies three primary "markers of erosion" in the L&D discourse:
1. **Semantic Dilution**: The replacement of "restitution" and "reparations" with "solidarity" and "cooperation."
2. **Institutional Encapsulation**: Placing L&D under adaptation frameworks to avoid independent fiscal authority.
3. **The Discretionary Trap**: Ensuring that all financial flows remain "invitational" rather than "obligatory," thereby neutralizing the polluter-pays principle.

## 6. Policy Recommendations

To move beyond the current impasse, the research suggests:
- **Revisiting the Governing Instrument**: Transitioning from a voluntary pledge model to a mandatory assessment model based on the principle of Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capacities (CBDR-RC).
- **Innovative Revenue Sources**: Implementing international solidarity levies on sectors like aviation and maritime shipping, which operate outside national liability constraints.
- **Direct Access Modalities**: Ensuring that the FRLD prioritizes direct access for local communities and indigenous peoples who are on the front lines of climate impacts.

## 7. Conclusion

The evolution of the Loss and Damage financing architecture is a testament to the power of discourse in shaping global policy. By systematically removing the language of liability, developed nations have created a financial system that offers "pity but not justice." True climate justice requires a return to the normative foundations of responsibility, ensuring that the FRLD becomes a vehicle for transformative change rather than a palliative measure for climate-induced displacement.
